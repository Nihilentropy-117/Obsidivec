# The Herbalist’s Dilemma

## A Tale of Miare

-----

## Chapter One: The Unlicensed Remedy

Tovan’s hands shook as he ground the feverfew leaves. The mortar and pestle had belonged to his grandmother, a licensed practitioner who had served the village of Kelton for forty years. She’d been dead three seasons now, and the license died with her.

The child on the pallet was burning with fever. Mira, the baker’s daughter, seven years old. Her father Brennan stood in the corner of Tovan’s cramped shop, wringing his flour-dusted hands. His wife Senna sat beside the pallet, dabbing their daughter’s forehead with a wet cloth that seemed to dry the moment it touched skin.

“The nearest licensed healer is in Forsetta,” Brennan said. “Two days ride. She won’t last.”

Tovan knew the law. Magic had been systemized in Miare, made accessible even to commoners for basic work. Cantrips. Low-level applications. But spells intended to affect another being’s physical or mental state required a license. Using such magic on an innocent non-license holder meant immediate arrest and trial.

The remedy he was preparing wasn’t purely mundane. The feverfew needed a touch of the Prime Essence to activate its deeper properties—just a whisper of the life force that permeated everything, the same energy that fueled all magic in the land. Every region acknowledged the Prime Essence, though they called it different things and drew upon it differently.

“I can’t,” Tovan said. “The law is clear.”

Brennan’s face contorted. “She’s dying. The guards—”

“Not the guards I worry about.”

They both knew what he meant. The Unfeeling Knights had been seen in the region lately. Those emotionless enforcers served the Unfeeling Moon, trained to suppress all feeling and follow the law without hesitation. If one of them caught wind of an unlicensed magical remedy, they wouldn’t care that a child’s life hung in the balance. If a violation was visible, they acted. They marked the accused with a moon-shaped symbol that burned into the face until the sentence was served.

Mira whimpered. Senna made a sound that wasn’t quite a sob.

Tovan looked at the child’s flushed cheeks, the sweat matting her dark hair. He thought of his grandmother’s hands, steady and sure, moving through the same motions he now performed. She’d trained him since he was younger than Mira. Taught him which roots to gather and when. Which combinations soothed and which harmed. How to feel the Prime Essence moving through living things, that subtle current beneath the surface of existence.

She’d wanted him to get licensed. He’d put it off. The examinations were in Forsetta, three days’ travel, and the fees weren’t trivial. There was always something more pressing. The shop needed repairs. The garden needed tending. His grandmother needed care as her own illness progressed.

And then she was gone, and the shop was his, and he told himself he’d get the license next season. Then the season after that.

Now a child was dying on his table and all his knowledge meant nothing because he’d never filled out the proper forms.

He kept grinding.

-----

## Chapter Two: A Question of Faith

The village shrine stood at Kelton’s edge, a modest stone structure housing small alcoves for the Housed Gods. Tovan’s grandmother had prayed to Bite of Fate—the god who embodied both medicine and disease, who understood that healing and harm were two sides of the same coin.

Tovan hadn’t prayed since she died. He went to the shrine anyway, more from habit than hope, and stood in the cool dimness while the afternoon light slanted through narrow windows.

An old woman knelt before one of the alcoves, humming something tuneless. She didn’t acknowledge him.

Tovan stood in silence. The painted eyes of the carved figures seemed to watch him from their alcoves, but they offered nothing. The gods of Miare lived in an adjacent plane, close enough to be felt but not close enough to answer desperate questions on demand.

The old woman stopped humming. “You’re Elsa’s grandson.”

He turned. She had risen, her joints creaking audibly. Her face was weathered, her eyes sharp.

“Yes.”

“She delivered my daughter forty years ago. Used magic to turn the baby when it wouldn’t come right. Had her license displayed on the wall the whole time.” The old woman smiled, toothless. “Law was satisfied. Child lived. Mother lived. Everyone happy.”

“I don’t have a license.”

“No.” She shuffled toward the door. “But you have hands. And you have what she taught you. The Prime Essence doesn’t care about paperwork, boy. It cares about intent.”

She left him standing there in the shrine’s quiet.

-----

## Chapter Three: The Knight at the Well

Tovan saw the Unfeeling Knight drawing water from the village well.

The armor was unmistakable—Moonsteel, forged from the essence of the Unfeeling Moon itself. It caught the light strangely, neither reflecting nor absorbing it. The knight’s face was visible beneath the raised visor: a woman, perhaps thirty, with features as still as carved stone. No expression. No warmth. No coldness either. Just absence.

Other villagers gave the well a wide berth. Unfeeling Knights made people nervous. Some towns banned them outright; others used them as their sole protection instead of training regular guards. Kelton fell somewhere in between—tolerant but wary.

Tovan’s feet carried him forward before his mind could object. “I need to ask you something.”

The knight lowered the bucket. Her eyes found his. There was nothing hostile in them, but nothing welcoming either. “Speak.”

“If a child is dying. And someone with knowledge but without a license uses magic to save them. What happens?”

“The law states that using hampering spells on an innocent non-license holder is grounds for immediate arrest and trial.”

“A healing spell isn’t hampering.”

The knight considered this. Her face revealed nothing of her thoughts—if she had thoughts in the way normal people did. The order trained that out of them, or so the stories said. “The law addresses spells intended to affect another being’s physical or mental state. Healing affects physical state. The license requirement applies.”

“So the child dies.”

“If no licensed healer is available, that is the outcome the law permits.”

Tovan felt heat rise in his chest. “That’s monstrous.”

The knight’s expression didn’t change. “The law exists to prevent harm from unqualified practitioners. A well-meaning fool can kill as easily as a malicious actor. The license ensures competence and accountability. This serves the greater balance.”

“And if the practitioner *is* competent? If they were trained by a licensed healer but never sought their own license?”

“Then they should seek a license.” The knight lifted her water bucket. “I am not here to enforce domestic matters unless violation is visible. I have seen nothing. I have heard a hypothetical question.” She began walking toward the inn where she was presumably staying.

Tovan watched her go. The Unfeeling Knights believed magic should not be used without good reason. They didn’t involve themselves in domestic affairs unless violation was clear and visible.

He had asked his question. She had answered with law. And she had told him, in her emotionless way, exactly what she intended to do about it.

Nothing. Unless she saw something.

-----

## Chapter Four: The Weight of Debt

Tovan returned to his shop as evening approached. The sun hung low, casting long shadows through the village streets. Brennan sat on the step outside, his head in his hands. He looked up when Tovan approached, and the hope in his eyes was almost unbearable.

“Any change?”

Brennan shook his head. “Senna’s still with her. She won’t eat. Won’t sleep. Just sits there, watching.”

Tovan went inside. The shop smelled of dried herbs and old wood, familiar scents that usually brought comfort. Now they felt accusatory. All these remedies lining the shelves, all this knowledge in his head, and none of it mattered because of a piece of paper he’d never obtained.

Senna looked up when he entered. Her eyes were red-rimmed, her face drawn. “Can you do anything? Anything at all?”

“The law—”

“I don’t care about the law.” Her voice cracked. “That’s my daughter. My only child. Do you understand what that means?”

Tovan understood. He’d watched his grandmother die slowly, painfully, from a wasting sickness that no magic could touch. He’d been helpless then too, but for different reasons. Some things were beyond any healer’s reach.

This wasn’t one of those things. This was a fever. Dangerous, yes, but treatable. His grandmother had handled dozens of similar cases. He’d assisted her on at least a score of them. He knew exactly what to do.

“I need to think,” he said. “Give me an hour.”

He went to the back room where he slept. It was small, barely large enough for a cot and a chest. The chest held his grandmother’s things—her journals, her tools, her focus crystal. He’d kept meaning to sort through it all, decide what to keep and what to sell.

He opened the chest and lifted out the crystal. It was the size of his thumb, pale blue, shot through with veins of white. A simple focus for channeling the Prime Essence. His grandmother had used it for decades. The edges were worn smooth from her grip.

Beneath the crystal lay her license. A sheet of vellum bearing the seal of the Healers’ Guild of Forsetta, her name inscribed in formal script, countersigned by three examiners. Worthless now. Non-transferable. The guild didn’t recognize bloodlines or apprenticeships. Every practitioner had to prove their competence individually.

Tovan held the license for a long moment. Then he set it aside and picked up one of her journals.

He’d read them before, in the months after her death. They documented forty years of practice—births and deaths, successes and failures, the mundane reality of a village healer’s work. But now he was looking for something specific.

He found it near the middle of the third journal. An entry from twenty-two years ago.

*Treated the miller’s boy today. High fever, shallow breathing, the same summer sickness that’s been moving through the village. The mother was hysterical. Father not much better. I gave them what comfort I could and set to work.*

*The working took most of the afternoon. His body was fighting hard but losing ground. I had to be careful—too much intervention can do more harm than good. The Prime Essence doesn’t like to be forced. But I found the balance eventually. Drew enough energy through the crystal to tip the scales, then let his own strength do the rest.*

*He’ll live. Another week of rest and he’ll be running through the wheat fields like nothing happened.*

*I sometimes wonder if I’m doing them a disservice, the families I help. They don’t see the work. They see the result and think it’s simple. Easy. They don’t understand how close their children come to death, or how much it costs to pull them back.*

*Maybe that’s for the best. Let them keep their innocence. The weight belongs on my shoulders, not theirs.*

Tovan closed the journal. His grandmother’s handwriting was neat, precise, the letters formed by a steady hand that never wavered.

His own hands were shaking.

-----

## Chapter Five: Preparations

He emerged from the back room as full darkness fell. Brennan had come inside, sat at the small table near the door. Senna still knelt beside Mira’s pallet, her lips moving in silent prayer.

“I’ll do it,” Tovan said.

Brennan’s head snapped up. “You will?”

“Yes. But I need you to understand the risks. If this goes wrong—if she dies despite my efforts—I’ll be blamed regardless of whether magic was involved. And if someone reports what I’ve done, I’ll be arrested. The knight in town will have no choice.”

“No one will report it.” Brennan stood, crossed the room, gripped Tovan’s shoulders. “I swear to you. I’ll make sure of it. Anyone who speaks against you will answer to me.”

“It’s not that simple. The law doesn’t care about intentions. Only outcomes.”

Senna spoke without turning from her daughter. “We’ll take that risk. All of it. Just save her. Please.”

Tovan nodded slowly. “I’ll need a few hours to prepare. The working has to be done properly or not at all.”

He moved to his workbench and began gathering materials. The feverfew paste he’d already prepared. Clean water from the well—he’d need to boil it first, let it cool. Strips of clean cloth for compresses. A small brazier for burning certain herbs; the smoke would help open the lungs. His grandmother’s focus crystal, now in his pocket, a comforting weight against his thigh.

And he’d need to prepare himself. The working would draw on his own reserves as much as the Prime Essence. He needed to be centered, calm, his mind clear of doubt and fear. His grandmother had always emphasized that. The Prime Essence responded to intent, and intent had to be pure.

“Brennan. I need you to go to the butcher and get me beef bones. Fresh, if he has them. I’ll make a broth—she’ll need nutrition when she wakes.”

“When she wakes,” Brennan repeated, clinging to the word. “Not if.”

“When.”

Brennan left. Tovan continued his preparations, moving through the familiar routine. He’d done this before, assisted his grandmother in this exact process. The difference was that she’d been there to guide him, to catch any mistakes, to step in if something went wrong.

Now he was alone.

Senna watched him work. After a while she spoke. “You loved her. Your grandmother.”

“Yes.”

“I could tell. The way you talk about her. The way you keep her things.” Senna glanced around the shop. “This place still feels like hers, doesn’t it?”

Tovan paused in his work. “Some days I forget she’s gone. I wake up and expect to hear her in the front room, grinding herbs or talking to a patient. Then I remember.”

“That’s how grief works. It doesn’t follow a straight line. It circles back when you least expect it.”

“You sound like you know.”

“I lost my mother when I was twelve. My father three years after that. Brennan’s family took me in.” Senna’s hand found Mira’s, held it gently. “She’s all I have left. Her and Brennan. If I lose her…”

She didn’t finish the sentence. She didn’t need to.

-----

## Chapter Six: The Working

The night deepened. Brennan returned with the bones. Tovan set them to simmer in a pot over the fire, adding salt and dried vegetables for flavor. The rich smell filled the shop, ordinary and comforting.

Mira’s condition hadn’t improved. If anything, she seemed worse—her breathing more labored, her skin taking on a grayish pallor that Tovan didn’t like. The fever was winning.

“It’s time,” he said.

He knelt beside the pallet, the focus crystal in one hand. His grandmother’s journal had described the technique, but he’d learned it first through observation, watching her hands move, feeling the subtle shift in the air when she drew on the Prime Essence.

In Miare, magic followed established rituals and formulae. It was ordered, structured, a dialogue between mortals and the divine built on respect and mutual understanding. The words mattered. The gestures mattered. But most of all, the intent mattered.

“I need you both to stay quiet,” Tovan said. “No matter what happens, don’t interrupt.”

Brennan nodded. Senna squeezed her daughter’s hand one more time, then released it and stepped back.

Tovan closed his eyes. The first step was to feel the Prime Essence—that underlying current of energy that permeated all things. It was always there, like a river running beneath the surface of reality. Most people never noticed it. Practitioners learned to sense it, to dip into it, to direct its flow.

He found it quickly. The Prime Essence responded to need, and the need in this room was overwhelming. It pressed against him, eager to be used, and he had to be careful not to draw too much too fast. That was how practitioners burned themselves out, or worse, caused damage to those they meant to help.

The working was simple in concept. He needed to strengthen Mira’s body, give it the edge it needed to fight the fever. Cool the inflammation in her blood. Ease the burden on her heart. He wasn’t curing her—magic couldn’t simply erase illness. He was tilting the balance, giving her body room to heal itself.

He spoke the words his grandmother had taught him. They weren’t spells in the traditional sense, not the elaborate incantations of the eastern mages or the ritualized chants of the western shamans. They were more like… prayers. Focused statements of intent, shaped to guide the energy where it needed to go.

The crystal grew warm in his hand. He placed his other hand on Mira’s forehead, feeling the heat of her fever, the rapid flutter of her pulse. He let the Prime Essence flow through him, from the crystal through his body and into hers. Not forcing it. Offering it.

Her body resisted at first. It didn’t recognize what he was doing, interpreted the influx of energy as another threat to fight. He had to be patient, gentle, let the working establish itself gradually.

Time lost meaning. He was aware of nothing but the connection between himself and the child, the steady pulse of energy, the subtle shifts in her condition. Her breathing eased slightly. Her pulse steadied. The fever didn’t break, but it loosened its grip, retreating from critical to merely dangerous.

He couldn’t do more. Not without risking damage. The working had reached its limit; the rest was up to her.

Tovan opened his eyes. The shop swam around him, and he had to grip the edge of the pallet to keep from falling. His head pounded. His hands trembled. He felt emptied out, wrung dry.

“Is it done?” Senna’s voice seemed to come from far away.

“Done as I can make it.” Tovan’s voice was hoarse. “Now we wait.”

-----

## Chapter Seven: The Long Night

They waited.

Brennan brought Tovan a bowl of the bone broth. He drank it mechanically, barely tasting it, his eyes on Mira’s face. The color was better—still flushed, but less gray. Her breathing had steadied into something closer to natural sleep.

“How long until we know?” Senna asked.

“By morning. If the fever breaks before dawn, she’ll recover. If it doesn’t…”

He didn’t finish. Senna understood.

The hours crept past. Tovan dozed in his chair, too exhausted to stay fully awake but too anxious to truly sleep. Brennan paced the length of the shop, back and forth, his footsteps a steady rhythm on the worn floorboards. Senna never moved from her daughter’s side.

Sometime after midnight, Mira stirred. Her eyes fluttered open, unfocused and confused. “Mama?”

Senna gasped. “I’m here, little one. I’m right here.”

“Thirsty.”

Tovan hauled himself upright. “Small sips. Just water at first.” He handed Senna a cup. “Hold her head up. Don’t let her drink too fast.”

Mira managed a few swallows before her eyes closed again. But she was sleeping now, truly sleeping, not the fevered unconsciousness of before. Her skin felt cooler to the touch.

“Is she—” Brennan started.

“Not yet. But this is good. This is very good.”

Dawn came slowly. The sky outside the shop’s windows lightened from black to gray to the pale pink of early morning. Birds began to sing. The village stirred to life—footsteps in the street, voices calling morning greetings, the creak of cart wheels on packed earth.

Mira woke again. Her eyes were clearer this time. She looked at her parents, at the unfamiliar surroundings, at Tovan standing by the workbench.

“Where am I?”

“The healer’s shop, sweetheart.” Senna was crying openly now, tears of relief streaming down her cheeks. “You’ve been very sick. But you’re going to be all right.”

“I’m hungry.”

Tovan laughed. The sound surprised him—rough and genuine, breaking through the tension that had held him all night. “Broth first. Then bread if you can keep it down. No solids until tomorrow at earliest.”

He heated a cup of broth and brought it to her. She drank it slowly, making faces at the taste, complaining that she wanted real food. Her parents watched with expressions of stunned gratitude.

The fever had broken. She would live.

-----

## Chapter Eight: Consequences

Tovan slept through most of the day. When he woke, the afternoon sun was slanting through his window, and someone was knocking at the shop door.

He pulled himself from the cot, every muscle aching, and shuffled to the front room. Mira was still there, sitting up now, arguing with her mother about whether she was well enough to go home. Brennan sat at the table, looking as exhausted as Tovan felt.

The knocking continued.

Tovan opened the door. A man stood on the step—middle-aged, well-dressed, with the bearing of someone accustomed to authority. Behind him stood two younger men in the simple tunics of town guards.

“Tovan, son of Maren, grandson of Elsa?”

Tovan’s stomach dropped. “Yes.”

“I am Aldric, magistrate of this district. I’ve received a report that you performed unlicensed magical healing on a child last night.”

The world seemed to tilt. Tovan gripped the doorframe for support. “Who reported it?”

“That’s not your concern. Is the report accurate?”

From inside the shop, Brennan’s voice: “What’s happening? Who’s at the door?”

Aldric looked past Tovan into the shop. His eyes found Mira, then moved to her parents. “I see the child in question is here. And alive, apparently.”

“Because of what I did.” Tovan found his voice. “She was dying. The nearest licensed healer is two days away. I did what I had to do.”

“That’s not for you to decide.” Aldric’s tone was neutral, neither sympathetic nor hostile. “The law exists for reasons. A magistrate’s duty is to uphold it.”

Brennan pushed past Tovan to face the magistrate. “This man saved my daughter’s life. Whatever law he broke, the result speaks for itself.”

“Results don’t determine legality. If they did, anyone could justify any action by pointing to a favorable outcome.” Aldric gestured to the guards. “You’ll need to come with us. There will be a hearing.”

Tovan’s mind raced. A hearing. That meant a formal process—charges laid, evidence presented, judgment rendered. The penalties for unlicensed magical practice ranged from fines to imprisonment to—in extreme cases—a permanent ban on practicing any form of magic at all.

“Can I at least gather my things?”

Aldric hesitated, then nodded. “Five minutes.”

Tovan went back inside. Senna was clutching Mira, her face pale with shock. Brennan followed him, still protesting to Aldric about the injustice of it all.

“Who reported me?” Tovan asked quietly as he gathered a few belongings into a satchel.

Brennan shook his head. “I don’t know. I told no one. Senna told no one.”

“Someone knew. Someone saw, or heard, or guessed.”

“I’ll find out. I swear to you, I’ll find out who did this, and they’ll answer for it.”

“Don’t.” Tovan put a hand on Brennan’s arm. “Whatever happens to me, don’t make it worse by starting a feud. Take care of your daughter. That’s what matters.”

He walked out of the shop and let the guards escort him to whatever awaited.

-----

## Chapter Nine: The Hearing

The hearing took place in Kelton’s small council hall, a stone building near the village center that served for meetings, disputes, and the occasional trial. Tovan had been inside a handful of times, always as a spectator. Now he stood at the front of the room, facing the magistrate’s bench.

Aldric sat behind the bench, flanked by two village elders. The arrangement wasn’t quite a formal court—Kelton was too small for that—but it had the weight of one. Whatever was decided here would be binding.

The hall was crowded. Word had spread quickly, as it always did in small villages. Tovan saw familiar faces in the audience: farmers he’d sold remedies to, merchants who’d traded with his grandmother, children he’d treated for minor ailments using purely mundane methods. They watched with expressions ranging from sympathy to curiosity to poorly disguised satisfaction.

Someone in this room had reported him. He didn’t know who.

“We are here to address the matter of unlicensed magical practice,” Aldric began. “The accused, Tovan of Kelton, is charged with performing a healing working on Mira, daughter of Brennan and Senna, without holding a valid practitioner’s license. How do you answer the charge?”

“I did it.” No point in denial. “The child was dying. I had the knowledge to save her. I used it.”

“The law does not make exceptions for good intentions.”

“Then the law is flawed.”

A murmur ran through the crowd. Aldric’s expression didn’t change. “Perhaps. But I am not empowered to change the law, only to apply it. Do you have anything else to say in your defense?”

Tovan thought about his grandmother. About her forty years of service, the hundreds of lives she’d touched. About the license on her wall that meant nothing now except as a reminder of what he’d failed to do.

“My grandmother trained me from childhood. I assisted her in her practice for fifteen years. I know what I’m doing. The only thing I lack is a piece of paper from the guild in Forsetta.”

“That piece of paper represents examination and verification of competence.”

“Does the fact that the girl lives not verify my competence?”

Another murmur. Aldric raised a hand for silence.

“Success in one case does not prove general competence. You might have been lucky. You might have done harm without realizing it, harm that will only manifest later. The examination process exists precisely to prevent such uncertainties.”

One of the village elders leaned forward—an old man named Garrett whom Tovan knew slightly. “If I may, magistrate. This young man’s grandmother was beloved in this village. She served us faithfully for decades. Is it not reasonable to assume that her training was sufficient?”

“Assumptions are not evidence,” Aldric said. “The law requires proof, not assumptions.”

“Then let him be tested.” The other elder, a woman named Petra. “If the guild in Forsetta can examine candidates, surely we can arrange something similar here. Test his knowledge. If he passes, let him be licensed. If he fails, then the law takes its course.”

Aldric considered this. “An irregular procedure.”

“These are irregular circumstances.”

The magistrate was silent for a long moment. Then he turned to Tovan. “Would you consent to such an examination?”

“Yes.” Without hesitation. “Test me however you like. I’ll prove my competence.”

“Very well. The hearing is adjourned until an examiner can be summoned from Forsetta. In the meantime, the accused will remain in Kelton under guard supervision. He is not to practice any form of magic, licensed or otherwise, until this matter is resolved.”

It wasn’t freedom, but it wasn’t prison either. Tovan allowed himself to breathe.

## Chapter Ten: The Informant

Three days passed. Tovan stayed in his shop, forbidden from practicing but allowed to sell mundane remedies—dried herbs, simple poultices, the kind of things anyone could prepare without touching the Prime Essence. A guard checked on him twice daily, morning and evening, to ensure he hadn’t fled.

He used the time to think. To prepare. And to wonder who had betrayed him.

The answer came on the fourth day.

The knock at his door was hesitant, almost apologetic. Tovan opened it to find a young man standing on the step—Derry, the son of the village’s wealthiest merchant. They’d never been friends, but they’d never been enemies either. Just two people who happened to live in the same small place.

“Can I come in?” Derry asked.

“That depends. Are you here to gloat?”

Derry flinched. “I’m here to apologize.”

Tovan stepped aside. Derry entered, looking around the shop as if seeing it for the first time. His hands fidgeted at his sides.

“It was you,” Tovan said. Not a question.

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Derry took a breath. “My father. He’s been trying to bring a licensed healer to Kelton for years. Says it would be good for business—a proper healer would attract people from the surrounding farms, and they’d spend money while they were here. Buy goods from his shop.”

“And I was competition.”

“Not exactly. You weren’t licensed, so you weren’t a real threat. But if you started practicing openly, even without a license, people might not see the need for a proper healer. Why pay guild rates when Elsa’s grandson will do it for less?”

Tovan felt cold anger settle in his chest. “So you reported me to eliminate the competition.”

“I didn’t think—” Derry’s voice broke. “I didn’t think it through. I just… my father was so pleased when I told him. Said I’d done well. That I was finally showing some business sense.” He looked at Tovan with something like desperation. “I didn’t know they’d actually arrest you. I thought you’d just get a warning, maybe a fine.”

“A fine I couldn’t pay. Which would have achieved the same result.”

“I know. I know.” Derry sank onto a stool. “I came here to tell you the truth because… because it’s eating me alive. The baker’s daughter almost died. You saved her. And I almost got you imprisoned for it.”

Tovan didn’t know what to feel. The anger was still there, but it was competing with something else—pity, maybe, or just exhaustion. Derry was weak. Easily manipulated by a father who saw everything in terms of profit and loss. That didn’t make what he’d done acceptable, but it made it comprehensible.

“What do you want me to say?”

“I don’t know. That you forgive me?”

“I don’t.”

Derry nodded as if he’d expected that. “Fair enough.”

“But I won’t hold it against you either. Not publicly. You’ve made your confession. Live with it however you can.”

“That’s more than I deserve.”

“Probably.” Tovan moved to the door, opened it. “Now leave. I have an examination to prepare for.”

Derry left without another word. Tovan stood in the doorway, watching him walk away, and tried to convince himself that the matter was settled.

It wasn’t, of course. Nothing was ever truly settled. But it was as close as he was likely to get.

-----

## Chapter Eleven: The Examiner

The examiner arrived six days later.

Her name was Verath, and she was not what Tovan expected. He’d imagined someone cold and bureaucratic, a guild functionary interested only in procedure. Instead, he found a middle-aged woman with laugh lines around her eyes and callused hands that spoke of practical experience.

“So you’re the one causing all the fuss,” she said when they met in the council hall. “Elsa’s grandson.”

“You knew my grandmother?”

“We trained together, forty-five years ago. Different paths after that—she came here, I stayed in the city—but we kept in touch.” Verath smiled. “She wrote to me about you. Said you had talent, but no ambition. That you’d rather dig in her garden than sit for examinations.”

Tovan felt a pang. “She wasn’t wrong.”

“No. She rarely was.” Verath set down a leather case on the table between them. “The magistrate has asked me to conduct a competency examination. If you pass, you’ll receive a provisional license allowing you to practice under supervision until you can complete the full guild examination in Forsetta. If you fail…”

“The law takes its course.”

“Just so.” Verath opened the case. Inside were various tools of the healer’s trade: crystals, vials, pouches of dried herbs, a set of surgical instruments. “Let’s begin with identification. Name every item in this case and describe its uses.”

The examination took most of the day. Verath was thorough but not harsh. She questioned him on herb lore and anatomy, on the theory behind magical healing and the practical considerations of its application. She had him demonstrate his technique on a training dummy—an enchanted construct that simulated various ailments without actually being alive.

Some questions he answered easily. Others made him pause, think, dredge up knowledge he hadn’t accessed in years. A few he couldn’t answer at all.

“That’s disappointing,” Verath said when he failed to identify a rare eastern herb. “But not surprising. Elsa’s training was excellent within its scope, but the scope was limited. Village healers don’t encounter everything.”

“Is that a failing grade?”

“Not necessarily. The guild doesn’t expect perfection. We expect competence within reasonable parameters.” Verath began repacking her case. “You have gaps in your knowledge, Tovan, but you have a solid foundation. More importantly, you understand the principles. The working you performed on the baker’s daughter—describe it for me.”

Tovan walked her through every step. The preparation, the centering, the way he’d drawn on the Prime Essence through his grandmother’s crystal. The careful balance between intervention and restraint. Verath listened without interrupting, her expression thoughtful.

“You were cautious,” she said when he finished. “Perhaps overly so. You could have pushed harder, shortened the recovery time.”

“I might have done damage.”

“Yes. But the fact that you considered that possibility, that you chose restraint over ambition—that’s what separates competent practitioners from dangerous ones.” Verath closed her case with a decisive click. “I’m going to recommend provisional licensing. You’ll need to complete the full examination within a year, and you’ll be subject to periodic review, but you’ll be legal.”

The relief was overwhelming. Tovan had to sit down before his legs gave out. “Thank you.”

“Don’t thank me. Thank your grandmother. She trained you well.” Verath stood. “One more thing. The guild is always looking for skilled practitioners in underserved areas. Kelton hasn’t had a proper healer since Elsa died. If you’re interested, we can discuss terms.”

“I’m interested.”

“Good. We’ll talk more after the paperwork is filed.” She headed for the door, then paused. “She would have been proud of you. Elsa. You should know that.”

She left. Tovan sat alone in the council hall, feeling something loosen in his chest that had been tight for months.

-----

## Chapter Twelve: Recovery

The provisional license arrived two weeks later. It came with a list of requirements—continuing education, record-keeping, periodic inspections—but none of that mattered as much as the simple fact of legitimacy. He was legal now. He could practice openly.

Word spread quickly. Within days, Tovan had more patients than he could easily handle. Some came for genuine ailments; others just wanted to see the man who’d defied the law and lived to tell about it. He treated them all, learning to distinguish the serious cases from the merely curious.

Mira came by often. She’d recovered fully, with no lasting effects from the fever, and had apparently decided that Tovan was the most interesting person in Kelton. She sat in his shop for hours, watching him work, asking endless questions about herbs and healing and magic.

“Will you teach me?” she asked one afternoon.

“You’re seven years old.”

“Grandmother said you were younger than me when you started learning.”

Tovan couldn’t argue with that. “Ask me again when you’re older. If you’re still interested, we’ll talk.”

Brennan and Senna came by too, usually bringing food or small gifts. They never mentioned the debt explicitly, but it hung in the air between them—a gratitude too large for words. Tovan accepted their offerings and tried not to feel uncomfortable about it.

The magistrate visited once, a formal follow-up to ensure the terms of Tovan’s provisional license were being observed. He seemed satisfied with what he found.

“For what it’s worth,” Aldric said before leaving, “I’m glad it worked out this way. The law is important, but so is common sense.”

“Thank you.”

“Don’t thank me yet. You still have a full examination to pass.”

Tovan smiled. “I’ll manage.”

-----

## Chapter Thirteen: The Merchant’s Shadow

Derry’s father was named Markon. He was a large man, florid and loud, known throughout Kelton for his business acumen and his ruthlessness in applying it. He had not been pleased by the outcome of Tovan’s hearing.

Tovan first noticed the signs a month after receiving his provisional license. Suppliers who had always dealt with his grandmother suddenly found reasons to deny him credit. Merchants who had stocked her remedies claimed they no longer had shelf space. A rumor began circulating that the healing he’d performed on Mira had actually been harmful, that she’d suffered some hidden damage that would manifest later.

None of it was provable. Markon was too clever for that. But the pattern was unmistakable.

“He’s trying to drive you out,” Brennan said when Tovan mentioned it. “Classic merchant tactics. Make the competition too expensive to maintain.”

“I’m not his competition. I’m a healer, not a merchant.”

“Doesn’t matter. His plan was to bring in a guild-licensed healer he could control—someone who’d owe him favors, kick back part of their earnings, direct patients to his shop for supplies. You ruined that plan by getting licensed yourself.”

“So now he’s punishing me for it.”

“That’s Markon. He never forgets a slight, real or imagined.”

Tovan considered his options. He had some savings—not much, but enough to weather a few lean months. If the pressure continued beyond that, he’d be in trouble.

“What would you do?” he asked Brennan.

“Me? I’d go to him directly. Lay cards on the table. Find out what it would take to make peace.”

“You think he’d negotiate?”

“Markon? Always. Everything’s a transaction with him. Figure out what you have that he wants, and offer a trade.”

It wasn’t the kind of confrontation Tovan would have chosen. But he was learning that running a practice involved more than just healing people. It involved navigating the complex web of relationships and interests that made up village life.

He sent word to Markon requesting a meeting.

-----

## Chapter Fourteen: The Transaction

They met in Markon’s shop—a sprawling establishment that sold everything from farming tools to luxury goods from the eastern continent. The merchant received Tovan in a back office cluttered with ledgers and samples, his bulk overwhelming the chair behind a massive desk.

“So,” Markon said. “The unlicensed healer who thinks he’s above the law.”

“The provisionally licensed healer who saved a child’s life.”

Markon waved a hand dismissively. “Semantics. You broke the rules. The fact that you escaped punishment doesn’t change that.”

“I’m not here to debate the past. I’m here to discuss the future.”

“Oh? And what future would that be?”

Tovan had spent two days preparing for this conversation. He’d consulted with Brennan, with the village elders, with anyone who might have insight into Markon’s motivations. A picture had emerged: a man driven by profit, yes, but also by something deeper. A need for control. For influence. For the certainty that came from being the most important person in any room.

“You want a healer in Kelton who works with you, not against you. Someone who directs patients to your shop for supplies, who enhances your reputation as a benefactor of the village.”

“That’s common knowledge.”

“Here’s what you might not have considered. A healer who owes his position to your patronage will always be seen as your tool. His credibility will be limited by that association. But a healer who came to that arrangement independently, after establishing his own reputation—that’s different. That’s a partnership of equals, which reflects well on both parties.”

Markon’s eyes narrowed. “You’re proposing an alliance.”

“I’m proposing a transaction. You stop undermining my practice. In return, I recommend your shop to my patients when they need supplies, and I publicly acknowledge your support of healing services in Kelton.”

“That’s all?”

“There’s one more thing. I want you to sponsor a healing fund—a pool of money that covers treatment costs for villagers who can’t afford them. It doesn’t have to be large. Just visible.”

Markon laughed. “You want me to give away money?”

“I want you to invest in goodwill. The fund bears your name. Every time someone benefits from it, they remember who made it possible. That’s worth more than the cost.”

The merchant was silent for a long moment. Tovan could almost see the calculations running behind his eyes: costs versus benefits, risks versus rewards.

“You’re cleverer than I gave you credit for,” Markon said finally. “Elsa never had a head for this kind of thing.”

“Elsa never needed one. She had forty years of reputation. I’m starting from scratch.”

“Fair point.” Markon stood, extending a hand. “You have a deal, healer. I look forward to a profitable partnership.”

Tovan shook the hand. It was like gripping a slab of meat.

As he left the shop, he wondered if he’d just made a wise decision or a terrible mistake. Probably both. That seemed to be how things worked in Kelton.

-----

## Chapter Fifteen: The Full Examination

Six months later, Tovan traveled to Forsetta for his full guild examination.

The city was larger than anywhere he’d been—a sprawling collection of districts and neighborhoods, each with its own character and customs. The Healers’ Guild occupied a compound near the center, a collection of buildings surrounding a courtyard where students practiced and examiners observed.

The examination took three days. Written tests on theory and pharmacology. Practical demonstrations of technique. Oral questioning by a panel of senior guild members who seemed determined to find the limits of his knowledge.

By the third day, Tovan was exhausted. He’d barely slept, surviving on nervous energy and the knowledge that everything he’d worked for hung in the balance. If he failed, he’d have to retake the examination in six months—and his provisional license would be revoked in the meantime.

The final test was a practical scenario. A simulated patient with multiple symptoms that could indicate several different conditions. Tovan had to diagnose the problem, propose a treatment, and justify his choices to the observing panel.

He drew on everything he knew. His grandmother’s teachings. Verath’s supplementary instruction during her periodic visits. The experience he’d gained treating actual patients in Kelton. The answers came slowly, but they came.

When it was over, the chief examiner—an ancient man named Corvin who had reportedly been practicing for sixty years—regarded him with an unreadable expression.

“You made some unconventional choices,” Corvin said.

“I drew on the techniques that seemed most appropriate.”

“Some of those techniques are not taught in guild curricula.”

“No. They came from my grandmother. She developed them over forty years of village practice.”

Corvin nodded slowly. “Elsa of Kelton. I remember her. She sat in that same chair, forty-five years ago, making the same argument.” He glanced at the other examiners. “We passed her despite her unconventional methods because we recognized their value. I see no reason to change that standard now.”

Tovan’s heart stopped. “You mean—”

“You pass. Congratulations, healer. Welcome to the guild.”

-----

## Chapter Sixteen: Homecoming

Tovan returned to Kelton a week later, full guild license in hand.

The village looked the same as when he’d left it—the same cluster of buildings around the central square, the same fields stretching toward the horizon, the same faces watching from windows and doorways. But something had changed in him. He saw the place differently now. Not as the small village where he’d grown up, where nothing ever happened and nothing ever would. As his home. The place where he belonged.

Brennan was the first to greet him. The baker had apparently been watching for his return, because he emerged from his shop the moment Tovan entered the square, flour still dusting his apron.

“Well?”

Tovan held up the license. “Full guild member. No more provisional status.”

Brennan whooped and grabbed him in a hug that lifted him off his feet. “I knew it! I told Senna, I said he’s going to pass, he’s Elsa’s grandson, of course he’s going to pass!”

Others gathered around—villagers who’d known him all his life, patients he’d treated over the past months, children who’d heard the story of how he’d saved the baker’s daughter. They congratulated him, shook his hand, slapped his back. Someone produced a jug of wine and started pouring cups.

Mira pushed through the crowd. She was taller now, her face losing some of its childish roundness. She looked at the license with wide eyes.

“Does this mean you can teach me now?”

Tovan laughed. “I said ask me when you’re older.”

“I’m older than I was when I asked before.”

“That’s not— never mind. We’ll talk about it.”

The impromptu celebration lasted until evening. By the time Tovan finally made it to his shop, he was pleasantly drunk and deeply tired. But there was one more thing he needed to do before he could rest.

He went to the back room. To his grandmother’s chest. He lifted out her old license—the vellum yellowed now, the ink faded—and held it for a moment. Then he set it aside and placed his new license next to it.

Two documents. Two generations. The same work continuing.

“I did it,” he said to the empty room. “I finally did it.”

There was no response, of course. His grandmother was gone. But somewhere, in the adjacent plane where the gods dwelt, or in whatever place the dead found after death, he liked to think she knew.

-----

## Chapter Seventeen: The Fever Returns

A year after Tovan’s licensing, the fever swept through Kelton in earnest.

What had struck Mira was only the first case—a scout, as it were, testing the village’s defenses. The full assault came without warning. Eleven villagers fell ill within the first week. By the second week, the number had doubled. By the third, Tovan was treating patients in shifts, sleeping in snatches between emergencies, pushing himself to the edge of collapse.

He sent word to Forsetta requesting assistance. The guild responded quickly—Verath herself came, bringing two junior healers and a cart full of supplies. They set up a temporary hospital in the empty granary, converting the space into a ward with rows of pallets and a central station for preparing treatments.

“This is a bad one,” Verath said as they worked side by side through another endless night. “I’ve seen this strain before. It swept through Forsetta two years ago. We lost thirty people before it burned itself out.”

“How do we stop it?”

“We don’t. We treat the symptoms, keep people alive long enough for their bodies to fight it off, and pray that the healthy ones don’t catch it.”

Tovan had been praying since the outbreak started. To Bite of Fate, to the Prime Essence, to anyone who might be listening. The prayers felt hollow—he was too tired for genuine faith—but he kept making them anyway. It was something to do while his hands worked and his mind ran in desperate circles.

Mira came to the granary every day. She’d grown into a serious child, watchful and quiet, absorbing everything she saw. Her parents had tried to keep her away, but she’d slipped past them repeatedly until they gave up.

“What can I do?” she asked Tovan during a brief moment when he wasn’t actively treating someone.

“You can stay healthy. That’s the most important thing.”

“I want to help. I want to learn.”

He looked at her—this child he’d saved a year ago, who’d apparently decided that her survival meant something, that she owed the world a debt she was determined to pay. She reminded him of himself at that age. Eager. Impatient. Certain that she could make a difference if only someone would give her the chance.

“Fine. Start by changing the water in those basins. Fresh water only—use the well behind the granary. Then bring clean cloths from that chest by the door. Can you do that?”

“Yes.”

“Then do it. And if you feel even slightly unwell—headache, fever, anything—you tell me immediately. Understood?”

She nodded and set to work. Tovan watched her for a moment, then turned back to his patients.

-----

## Chapter Eighteen: The Turning Point

The fever peaked three weeks after it started.

On the worst night, Tovan lost two patients within an hour of each other. An old man named Gareth who’d lived in Kelton his entire life, and a young woman named Isla who’d just moved to the village with her husband. Different ages, different circumstances, the same disease.

He stood between their pallets after covering their faces, too exhausted to feel anything clearly. Verath found him there.

“You need to rest.”

“I can’t. There are still—”

“The others will keep for a few hours. You’ve been awake for two days straight. If you collapse, you’ll be no good to anyone.”

“I can’t,” he repeated. “Not while they’re dying.”

Verath took his arm, guided him to a chair in the corner. “Listen to me. I’ve been doing this for thirty years. I’ve seen outbreaks worse than this one. And I’ve learned that the only way to survive them is to pace yourself. The sick need healers who can function, not heroes who burn out and fall over.”

“Is that what I’m trying to be? A hero?”

“I don’t know. Maybe. Or maybe you’re trying to prove something—to yourself, to your grandmother’s memory, to the village that almost saw you imprisoned. Whatever it is, it won’t help anyone if you kill yourself pursuing it.”

Tovan sagged in the chair. His eyes burned. His hands were raw from constant washing. “I don’t know how she did it. My grandmother. Forty years of this.”

“One day at a time. That’s how anyone does anything.” Verath pressed a cup of water into his hands. “Drink. Rest. I’ll wake you if anything critical happens.”

He drank. He rested. And the next morning, when he returned to work, the fever had begun to recede.

-----

## Chapter Nineteen: Aftermath

Kelton lost seven people to the fever. It should have been more. The village council erected a small marker at the village shrine, listing the names of the dead. The surviving families gathered there to mourn, to remember, to begin the slow process of moving on.

Tovan attended the memorial ceremony. He stood at the back of the crowd, watching the faces of people he’d treated, people he’d saved, people he hadn’t been able to save. The weight of it settled on his shoulders like a physical burden.

Mira found him afterward. She’d been helping throughout the crisis, fetching water and carrying supplies, doing whatever small tasks she could manage. She looked older than her eight years now—serious and sad and strangely composed.

“You did everything you could,” she said.

“Did I? Some days I’m not sure.”

“My grandmother used to say that doubt is the companion of wisdom. Only fools are certain of everything.”

Tovan smiled despite himself. “Your grandmother sounds like she was a wise woman.”

“She was. She died in the fever.” Mira’s voice was steady, but her eyes glistened. “I wanted to save her. I tried to help. But I didn’t know enough.”

“I’m sorry.”

“Don’t be sorry. Teach me.” The girl looked up at him with an intensity that bordered on fierce. “Teach me so that next time, I can do more than carry water.”

Tovan considered his options. She was young—too young, by traditional standards. But traditional standards hadn’t accounted for children who’d survived deadly fevers and then spent three weeks helping treat others. Traditional standards hadn’t accounted for a village that would need more than one healer to serve its needs.

“Ask your parents,” he said. “If they agree, we’ll start with the basics. But I warn you—it’s not glamorous work. Most of what I do is grind herbs and clean wounds and tell people things they don’t want to hear.”

“I know. I’ve been watching.”

“Then you know what you’re signing up for.” Tovan extended his hand. “Welcome to the practice, apprentice. Try not to make me regret this.”

Mira shook his hand with all the solemnity of a guild ceremony. “I won’t.”

-----

## Chapter Twenty: The Village Healer

Five years passed.

Tovan’s practice grew. He took on two more apprentices besides Mira—a farmer’s son with a talent for herb lore, and a merchant’s daughter who’d discovered she had an unusual affinity for the Prime Essence. Together, they served not just Kelton but the surrounding farms and hamlets, traveling when necessary, treating whoever needed help.

The shop expanded. Tovan bought the building next door and converted it into a proper treatment space, with separate rooms for examination and recovery. Markon contributed to the renovation—the healing fund had grown into a genuine institution, and the merchant’s reputation as a benefactor had grown with it.

The Unfeeling Knights passed through Kelton occasionally. They never troubled Tovan. His license was in order, his records immaculate, his practice beyond reproach. Whatever ambiguity had surrounded his first healing of Mira had long since been resolved by years of legitimate work.

One spring morning, Tovan was grinding herbs in the back room—still using his grandmother’s mortar and pestle, worn even smoother now—when Mira burst through the door.

“There’s a child in the square. High fever. They just brought her in from one of the outer farms.”

Tovan set down his work and followed her out.

The child was four years old, small and pale, her breathing shallow and rapid. Her parents hovered nearby, their faces tight with fear. Tovan knelt beside her, placing a hand on her forehead, feeling the familiar signs of the summer sickness.

“We’ll need to move her to the treatment room,” he said. “Mira, prepare the standard fever protocol. I’ll start the examination.”

Mira nodded and hurried away. She was fifteen now, nearly as skilled as Tovan himself, ready to take her guild examination in the fall. In a few years, she’d be a full healer—the second one Kelton had produced in two generations.

Tovan lifted the child gently and carried her into the shop. The parents followed, their fear giving way to something that looked almost like hope.

“Will she be all right?” the mother asked.

Tovan had learned not to make promises. Too much could go wrong, too many factors lay beyond any healer’s control. But he’d also learned that sometimes people needed reassurance more than accuracy.

“She’s in good hands,” he said. “We’ve treated this before. We know what to do.”

He laid the child on the examination table and set to work. The Prime Essence responded to his touch, familiar now as breathing. The mortar and pestle waited on the shelf, ready for whatever preparations the treatment required.

Outside, the village of Kelton carried on. Farmers worked their fields. Merchants haggled in the square. Children played in the streets, their laughter drifting through the open window.

It was an ordinary day. An ordinary village. An ordinary life.

Tovan wouldn’t have had it any other way.
