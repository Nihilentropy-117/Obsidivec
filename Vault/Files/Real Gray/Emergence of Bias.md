

I think that human behavior is largely a function of human aggregateness. What do I mean by that?

Human behavior is dependent on how many of the bastards you’ve got laying around.

If you have one human in a vacuum, by themselves, they are a pretty reasonable sort of person the majority of the time. Removed from any external social stimulus, they’re more often than not going to be a normally reasonable person.

If you get two people, though, well, then they’re going to find things they agree on and they’re going to find things they disagree on. But the things they find that they agree on are going to be reinforced—they’re going to hold those opinions even stronger. Inverse to the things they disagree on, they’re going to not feel as strongly about because they don’t have that reinforcement.

So even as a two-person grouping, you are going to see the first hints of prejudices and whatnot start to emerge, just as a result of two people.

But what about four people? Eight people? Sixteen people? Thirty-two people? A hundred? A thousand?

The greater the mass of people gathered, inherently the more reinforcements are going to occur. The more inherent, even unconscious, biases and prejudices are going to be reinforced and emergent patterns are going to occur.

Ten thousand people. A million people.

How many people does it take? How many people does it take before we start organizing ourselves into large enough groups that we can create deniability? That we can create the idea that subgroups are formed in which some are better than others?

This is going to happen. This always happens. Every time that critical mass is reached.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
