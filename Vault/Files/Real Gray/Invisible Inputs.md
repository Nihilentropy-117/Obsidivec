

I started out by calling it a sixth sense, but I don’t know if it necessarily qualifies as a sense. I also don’t really know what to call it. The best I have right now is causality or something.

But whatever it is, it’s a very potent human skill: the ability to take a set of inputs and inherently predict an output.

This can be over a very small scale—like someone building a Rube Goldberg machine for marbles or bouncing a couple of pool balls off each other—to running massive multinational corporations. But in order to use this ability, we plug in data from our five senses and out comes a hunch or an educated guess or something.

But we know that there is data in the world that is not accessible immediately by our five senses. We know that there is data that is part of the five senses that we can’t even access. There’s smells we can’t smell. There’s colors we can’t see, not even to bring up electromagnetic forces, gravity, and all of the other natural phenomena for which we don’t even have an organ designated for sensing.

We do know that a lot of these are, in fact, perceivable by humans to some degree via some combination of existing senses and a little bit of guesswork. We have the occasional human that can echolocate or see a slightly different range of colors.

So why couldn’t we use the data that we’re not actually aware of knowing in causality?

A common human experience is to turn to look at the phone before it rings, or to walk over to the window just to see someone approaching the door, to turn to look in an empty room and find out there’s actually someone there, even though we’re not aware of receiving any of these things.

Is that a coincidence? It seems too common.

Is it possible that we’ve figured out how to detect the electromagnetic frequencies of an incoming phone call? Have we figured out how to detect the subsonic frequencies of a frequent visitor’s car engine that we can’t actively pick up on, but something does?

Who knows. I’d like to find out, though.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
