

Don’t rock the boat. Don’t start stuff. Don’t mix it up. That’s the way we’ve always done it. It can’t be done. It’s impossible. The guy before me tried that, and he said it didn’t work.

Well, what do you mean, have I tried it? No. Why would I have tried it? It doesn’t work.

No. Why would I have tried it? That’s not the way we do it.

All of these are against progress. All of these are the opposite of how humanity advances. Sure, they may be true. Sure, you may know what you’re talking about. In fact, the odds are you probably do know what you’re talking about. The odds are that it probably is impossible. It probably can’t be done. There’s probably a really good reason for why we do it the way we do it. And very often that reason is written in blood.

But sometimes you just have to know. Sometimes you just have to poke and prod and try and understand why we don’t do things that way.

Part of this is, of course, the vital skill of being able to differentiate which rules are written in blood and which rules are just written in bureaucratic red ink. But from my experience, after a while, you learn to differentiate. You learn an inherent idea of what bits of life are open to being fucked with and which ones are solid.

Doesn’t mean I’m right all the time. Doesn’t mean I’m even usually right. It just means I’ve gotten real good at not poking stuff that hurts me too bad.

But I like to know why. I like to know why it doesn’t work, why it does work. Sometimes you gotta take stuff apart. You gotta break stuff, make mistakes. As Ms. Frizzle said, get messy.

And yeah, it means that at the point in my life that I’m writing this, I don’t have a lot of money. There hasn’t been a whole lot of opportunity for that, because I jump. I always take the leap. I always take the risk, just to see what happens.

‘Cause that’s living. That’s the golden path. That’s a surprise, right? That’s what makes this human—the ability to take actions even while not knowing what’s gonna happen next.

That’s why I always jump. That’s why I always make the leap. ‘Cause it might be cliché, but you miss 100% of the shots you don’t take.

And like I said, I don’t claim to be perfect. I’ve definitely made a lot of mistakes. I’ve gotten it wrong definitely more than I’ve gotten it right. But I’m fine with that, because the places I’ve been, the things I’ve seen, the people I’ve met, the real-world life experiences that I’ve had—some of them were good, some of them were bad, some of them were absolutely terrible. But someone has to have them. Someone has to have bad times, and someone has to have the good times.

But I don’t know that with that attitude it’s possible to have a regret.

Are there things that if I were to do them again today, I would do differently? Absolutely. The majority of it. I would do the majority of things differently. But the paradox: I would do them differently because I saw how it happened the first time. If I went in blind to these things again, if I went in blind through my life again, I’d make the same fucking choices for the most part, I think. Because the knowledge, the skill base, the combination of memories and instincts that had been built up were built up by the bad decisions, by getting it wrong.

In order for me to not make those bad mistakes, in order for me to make better decisions in the future—would it have been easier from the beginning just to accept the generalities, accept the common sense, accept the not-so-common sense that people told me did and did not work? Absolutely. That would have, without a doubt, allowed me to lead a much less stressful and more successful life.

But I wouldn’t have my memories. I wouldn’t have my experiences. I wouldn’t have what I consider to be a fairly in-depth understanding of exactly how the world actually works under the hood. If you take all of its clothes off and shine a bright light on it, there’s machinery underneath. There’s a way that people work. There’s a way that society works.

And I like to think that by all my breaking shit, all my getting it wrong, all my fucking around, I have a—if not good, at least passable—true understanding of what’s underneath.

I guess the real test, of course, is going to be whether I can do anything with that knowledge now that I’ve obtained it. ‘Cause who really knows? I won’t know for sure until I’m lying on my deathbed looking back.

But I think I can make it. I think I’ll be able to get where I want to be and be able to look back and say, “Fuck y’all, I did it my way.” Yeah, sure, it was harder. But it’s my own path. I made my own road. And probably to the vast majority of people would say, “What’s the point? Why bother?”

But I know that there’s other people out there that feel the same way.

And this is for y’all.
