
Belief begets reality—my old mantra. It still holds up, but there’s a certain disingenuity to it.

Belief begets reality, while perfectly adequate for my own needs, especially in the early stages of the project, implies that belief actually begets reality. And it doesn’t. I can’t just believe something and it becomes true. I’m not talking about flying or telekinesis or shooting fireballs. I’m talking about its actual connotations in interpersonal dynamics and the narrative.

It’s not simply enough to believe in a new narrative in order to forge it. There’s an aspect of action as well. You must not only believe—or possibly, even in lieu of belief, you must act like you believe. You must jump. You must, with 100% of your being and 100% of the actions you take, act as if the narrative you’re seeking has already come to pass or is rapidly approaching with all certainty.

This does not, of course, guarantee anything. But it is by far the most effective method of persuading the world around you to do your bidding.

It is, I believe, why the reflective vest and clipboard technique works so well. There’s one thing the vast majority of human beings are either unprepared, unwilling, or unable to deal with: someone who looks like and acts like they know what they’re doing.

This is, I believe, for a variety of reasons—whether an inherent unwillingness to question what is perceived as authority or a reluctance to interfere in systems beyond them. Nobody wants to be the guy who doesn’t obey the man in a reflective vest directing traffic. The potential consequences of questioning authority in that particular situation are far greater than the consequences of making a left turn when you didn’t absolutely have to. The consequences of denying the legitimacy of a health inspector are far greater than the consequences of letting a random man with a clipboard wander around the kitchen of your restaurant for five minutes.

There are, of course, exceptions. These rules do not apply to everyone. But I seem to be realizing that the people for whom these techniques do not work are, in the vast majority of cases, the ones using them.

This is very interesting and needs to be looked into more.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
