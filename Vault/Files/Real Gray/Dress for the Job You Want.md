Dress for the job you want, not for the job you have. Or, alternatively, dress for the job you’re interviewing for… no, dress for the job you want, not the job you’re interviewing for. I’ve heard that too. But I like dress for the job you want, not the job you have. That’s one of those sayings, isn’t it? One of those things I really wish someone had told me a long time ago—and really hammered in. I’m sure I’d been hearing it for years, but I wish that, early on, someone had sat down with me and explained what it meant and why it mattered. I didn’t pick up on it until way later in life than I would have liked.

It’s a multifaceted thing. It’s about self-respect. It’s about comfort. It’s about how you approach the world.

There’s also that other quote—I don’t remember the exact wording—something like: how you do one thing is how you do all things. Basically, do everything well or do nothing well. So if you dress for the job you have, you’re committing to doing that well. That’s probably where the whole “make your bed every day” thing comes in. It’s about doing everything properly.

You start at the bottom, at the entry-level job, the fast-food place or retail. You can get away with wearing old, frayed Metallica shirts. And that separates out the chaff. Just because you’re allowed to wear shitty clothes—just because you’re allowed not to bathe or dress yourself—doesn’t mean you should. The way you do one thing reflects the way you do all things.

So when you show up to a job interview overdressed, it makes a statement. When you show up to your job every day overdressed, it makes a statement. It says something about self-respect and about how you live your life—clean, put together. It tells people something about you.

For the longest time, I thought it was just a rules thing. Like, this is the outfit the powers that be have decided looks good. This is what the arbitrary people in charge say people should wear. And I guess that’s still true—fashions come and go—and I still don’t see why a shirt with a collar is inherently more “put together” than a shirt without one. But that’s the fashion of our time. The real statement is that you’re put together enough to know the fashion of the time, and that you care enough about yourself and your place in society to follow it. Whether it makes sense or not.

It’s not exactly bending to authority. In a way it still is, but you can make your own fashion to a degree. You can do something different. You can be a rebel. You just have to obey the basic rules. Whatever you do, do it well. Be neat. Give the impression that you’ve put yourself together the same way you approach the rest of your life—work, love, raising your kids—in a correct, put-together manner.

Because it’s confidence. That’s how you show confidence: being put together, knowing what you’re doing, knowing what you’re saying. Knowing you do things well. And being known by others as someone who does things well and is reliable gives you something real to be confident in.

Confidence only gets you so far if you haven’t earned it. You can fake a lot, but eventually the bottom falls out if you don’t have the metal to back it up.

I just wish I’d known how to begin a long time ago. Because you really do have to dress for the job you want.
