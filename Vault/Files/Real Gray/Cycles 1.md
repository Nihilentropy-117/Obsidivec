
Cycles. We need to talk about cycles.

As Robert Evans said: the one thing I’ve learned from history is that people never learn anything from history. Look back at the last 200 years—a time period for which we have a lot of data—and a series of repeated occurrences seem to be happening. The question is whether there’s a particular pattern to these cycles or if it’s just a generally repeating sequence.

First step: identify what the cycles are.

The first one that comes to mind is what I’ll call the progressive-conservative cycle. Bringing modern political parties and terminology into this isn’t my goal—the current state of the political system across the world is just a projection of these underlying concepts. Except in the most modern sense, the parties themselves are irrelevant. So we’ll go with progressive and conservative.

**The Cycle**

Let’s start with a major event. You have a society that is functional in some way. It’s been uninterrupted for some time. Then, slowly, the conservative half of society—and it will be half, it’s always half, it’s always been half, and it seems like it always will be half because of Gaussian distribution or something—begins to conserve. Which includes wealth.

The progressive half of society, by definition, doesn’t conserve wealth. I don’t mean progressives don’t have savings accounts or retirement plans. But they don’t have the same need to exponentially grow their net worth. This is a generalization—there are people on both sides who don’t follow this because humans are like that—but as a general rule, progressives devote excess capital to projects. In the best of times, societal projects. In the worst of times, personal projects, but still projects. In the worst of times, progressives dedicate excess capital to businesses. In the best possible situation, they devote it to feeding the hungry. The point is: progressives tend to devote extra capital and resources to things larger than themselves or their direct family group, while conservatives pull excess capital inward, building personal and family wealth exponentially.

Unfortunately for society as a whole, this tends to have a funneling effect—moving fiscal conservatives upward and fiscal progressives downward. If one group tends to be exothermic and one group tends to be endothermic, energy can only flow one way. If you have a conservative system and a progressive system, capital can only flow one way.

This accumulation of wealth by the conservative half of society eventually moves the bar—the defining boundary between the two systems. Instead of the division of wealth being a 50-50 split, there’s no longer enough resources to sustain that. It becomes a 60-40 split, with the 40 having more capital. Then when the 60% can no longer support the 40%, it becomes a 70-30 split. Then 80-20. Possibly 90-10. Eventually you have a situation where the top 1-5% of people have 95% of the wealth, and 95% of people have 5% of the wealth. The amount of wealth and capital accumulated by the top percentages can only be sustained by the continued efforts of 95% of the population.

I believe that’s where we are in the cycle right now, give or take.

**The Breaking Point**

In previous times, as I believe will happen now, the next step is that the majority is pushed too far. The majority—especially historically the middle class—eventually has too much taken away from them. This is the skilled laborers. The college-educated laborers. The middle class—the ones society rests almost its entire load on in an optimal society. This should be the 80%. Eighty percent should occupy the middle class with 10% buffers on each end, lower and upper. Those numbers aren’t exact—I’m not an economist—but the split society is at in this state is unsustainable.

We have the promise of exponential growth, infinite growth. Neither is sustainable. People invest in stock thinking it’s going to grow forever, but the economy cannot grow forever. Perhaps the economy as a metaphor, as a whole unit, can grow infinitely because that’s how money works. But if you take a single company—Walmart, Amazon, whoever—the stockholders demand infinite growth. They demand 9% year after year after year. That isn’t feasible. Eventually Amazon will have sold their product to everyone who wants to buy it. Eventually no one else will want Amazon Prime, and the people who have it will purchase a consistent amount of goods every year. And I don’t know if anyone can tell, but that’s not growth. That’s a constant.

It’s a constant battle to increase profit either by trimming off the bottom or by obtaining new customers. And I don’t know if anyone else has noticed, but I’m personally less likely to buy a new service if the quality has been steadily decreasing. You can’t pull off the bottom and expect to grow on the top at the same time. Eventually that tension is going to be too much for the system to handle, and the entire thing is going to collapse.

So we have an implicitly agitated middle class and an increasingly volatile economic structure that the entire thing is built on. Neither of these are new things. Sure, the stock market is new, relatively speaking, but the economic principles involved and the actual things people do about the problems haven’t changed.

That’s the cycle. It raises the bar for a sustainable life until just staying alive is the only thing people can financially do. Eventually even that becomes unfeasible. Once the bar gets so high, they overthrow. Whether with machine guns, like the Battle of Blair Mountain and the unions in West Virginia in the early 1900s. Whether the French Revolution. Whether picketing. Whether, as it seems in the modern era, turning up to vote. But there will be a revolution. And when that happens, it will be—at least historically—quick, fast, and decisive.

I do not think this has begun yet, although the whispers are present.

**The Revolution and Recovery**

Once the revolution occurs, the progressive party will be rapidly thrust to the top. At this point, they’ll make rapid social and economic changes and drop the floor out from under the economic conservatives. The economic conservatives will in turn clutch their pearls and start yelling loudly, unable or unwilling to cope with the fact that this entire thing was their fault for accumulating too much wealth. They’ll find someone to blame. In Germany, they blamed the Jews. In the Russian Revolution, I think they also blamed the Jews—because Jews weren’t allowed to do a lot of work, but Christians weren’t allowed to lend money, so Jews became bankers. Then any economic problems were blamed on the Jews because all the bankers were Jews, but it was because it was the only job they were fucking allowed to have.

Point is: they blame someone. They blame someone on the progressive side.

The progressives make rapid socioeconomic changes. Society evens out and recovers.

Unfortunately, it is historically shown that progressives are horribly bad at governing. They have absolutely no ability to form a cohesive government unit. Look at Spain with the anarchists, the communists, and the socialists. Look at Britain around the same time with the same groups. Italy. Germany. Any place where fascism and communism or fascism and anarchism have clashed violently in recent history. The one thing we’ve shown is that the fascists or the conservatives can, in times of need, all group together and fight as one cohesive governmental unit or one cohesive rebellious unit. The progressive forces can never manage this, at least not for any sustained period of time. They fracture. They want too many different things.

This fracturing occurs over time, and the progressive government spreads out and loses steam. The conservative movement starts again. They quiet down, start conserving wealth, start building up capital. Eventually they have enough capital to control the system once again by default.

And so the cycle repeats. Over and over and over. From the Roman Empire to the present day. Germany, Italy, Britain, Russia, Spain, Portugal, Brazil. All over the world. The same cycle, over and over again.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
