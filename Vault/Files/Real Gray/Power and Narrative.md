**Memo: Narrative Perception and Manipulation as Power**

We need to talk about the power of strong inherent social skills—specifically, people who can tell the story in the way others want to hear it. These are people who can see the narrative for what it truly is.

Someone who can both see the narrative as it is *and* process it—who understands how people receive narratives and can determine what got those narratives presented to them in the first place—would be very powerful indeed. This is inherently understood, which is why “controlling the narrative” is already a phrase. But I don’t think we pay enough attention to what that actually entails.

We have people—possibly a large number—who understand the narrative as it is, whatever that is. We also have a large number who have no idea what the narrative actually is, no inherent skill there, but who do inherently know how to present whatever narrative they *do* see to other people correctly. These are the demagogues, manipulators, charlatans—people capable of exerting enormous control over others through charisma and persuasive energy, not by representing the narrative truthfully, but by creating their own and forcing it down people’s throats.

Which raises the question: what sort of person was Hitler? Did he see the world as it really is and have the skill to reframe it through his own twisted lens? It depends on whether you believe your own bullshit. There’s a difference between genuinely believing something and pushing it through sheer force of will versus recognizing its utility as a mechanism without actually believing any of it.

This means there are several groups of people:

1. People who can neither see nor manipulate the narrative
2. People who can see the narrative as it truly is but have no ability to manipulate it
3. People who can manipulate the narrative as they understand it but have no ability to see what it actually is—they just insert whatever serves their interests, creating a false narrative
4. People who can both see and manipulate the narrative

That fourth group could do so legitimately or by injecting their own narrative, but they have both capabilities.

The most common type is the first group—people who can neither see the narrative nor manipulate it. This is the average person who goes about their day, not knowing, not caring. No inherent orientation toward understanding how any of this works.

We should talk about the people at the top—the fourth group. People who are both aware and capable. I have suspicions this may be an immensely powerful lens, and looking at it from this perspective, it should have been obvious from the beginning. But only recent events made me consider it at all.

I think we can reference the niceness versus usefulness factor here. Actually, it shouldn’t be the usefulness-to-likability factor. It should be productivity versus ability to manipulate the narrative in your favor. Really, it’s just the ability to manipulate the narrative in your favor that’s going to be primary—the other factor might prove irrelevant. Does it matter how productive you are as long as you control the narrative?

We’re going to have to classify and observe more about this. And we need to define exactly what the narrative *is* first. Based on research so far, we’re getting some good ideas as to what it is and how to define it. But I don’t have a solid, contextual answer I feel confident enough to use to describe it from scratch to someone completely unfamiliar with my research.

That needs to be the next thing: determine what the hell we’re actually talking about in a form that can be chronologically and scientifically defined, understood, and explained to others.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
